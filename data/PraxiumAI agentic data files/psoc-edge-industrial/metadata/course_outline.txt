### Section 1.1: Understanding the PSOC Edge E8 Architecture (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll orient yourself to the chip's multi-domain block diagram, understand which blocks live in which domain, and see how this architecture directly shapes your firmware partitioning decisions for the face recognition terminal.

---

#### Subsection 1.1.1: Map the Dual-Domain Architecture

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Annotated interactive block diagram (Figure 1 from datasheet)

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 organizes its blocks into three domains: the High-Performance Domain (CM55 + Ethos-U55 + GPU + SMIF + USB), the Low-Power Domain (CM33 + NNLite + SCBs + I3C + Crypto + Ethernet + CAN FD), and the Deep Sleep / Always-On Domain (autonomous analog, LP comparators, SCB0)
- The High-Performance Domain is designed for burst workloads — DSP, neural network inference, display output — and can be selectively powered down between recognition events
- The Low-Power Domain is "virtually always-on": it handles security, communications, access control I/O, and lightweight ML inference continuously
- The Deep Sleep / Always-On Domain keeps running even when both CPU domains are asleep, enabling wake-on-event from analog sensors or the SCB0 I2C/SPI slave
- The IPC block bridges the two CPU domains, allowing the CM55 and CM33 to synchronize tasks and share data without polling
- For your face recognition terminal: the CM55 domain handles face detection inference and display rendering; the CM33 domain handles access control I/O, secure key management, and presence detection via NNLite

#### Subsection 1.1.2: Identify Key Compute Blocks

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Comparison table + short video walkthrough (3 min video + 4 min reading)

**Content:**
- The Cortex-M55 runs at up to 320 MHz in HP mode and 140 MHz in LP mode; it includes Helium DSP extensions, FPU, 32 KB I/D caches, and 512 KB of 0-wait-state TCM — making it your primary compute engine for face recognition inference
- The Cortex-M33 runs at up to 160 MHz in HP mode and 80 MHz in LP mode; it has a 16 KB I-cache and 1 MB of coupled SRAM — your always-on security and control processor
- The Ethos-U55 NPU runs at the same clock as the CM55 (up to 320 MHz) and delivers 128 MACs/clock cycle with 8-bit weight support and weight compression — roughly 50× faster than a Cortex-M7 at the same frequency for NN inference
- The NNLite coprocessor runs at the same clock as the CM33 (up to 160/80 MHz) and accelerates a specific set of NN kernels optimized for low-to-moderate complexity models; it operates in the Active state and is ideal for always-on presence detection
- Both CPUs support SWD and JTAG debug; the chip also supports boundary scan via a separate TAP controller
- Key implication for your terminal: you have two independent inference engines — choose based on model complexity, latency requirement, and power budget (covered in Module 3)

#### Subsection 1.2.2: Apply PDL for Peripheral Setup

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Annotated code walkthrough scenario (text-based with pseudocode)

**Module Overview:** Power management is where face recognition terminals live or die on battery-backed installations. ### Section 2.1: System Power Modes and Domain Control (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll learn to select the right system power mode for each terminal operating state and understand how the HP/LP domain structure affects what your firmware can do at each frequency.

---

#### Subsection 2.1.1: Select System Power Modes

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Interactive decision tree + reference table

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 supports four system power modes: **System HP** (core at 0.9 V, all peripherals at maximum speed — CM55 up to 320 MHz, CM33 up to 160 MHz), **System LP** (core at 0.8 V, reduced speed — CM55 up to 140 MHz, CM33 up to 80 MHz), **System Deep Sleep** (CPUs halted, most peripherals off, SRAM optionally retained), and **System Hibernate** (lowest power, GPIO state frozen, wakeup via reset or wakeup pin)
- For your face recognition terminal: use **System HP** during active face recognition (CM55 + Ethos-U55 running at full speed); use **System LP** during idle monitoring (CM33 + NNLite running presence detection at reduced power); use **System Deep Sleep** between recognition events
- The buck regulator powers core logic at 0.9 V (HP), 0.8 V (LP), or lower in deep sleep — dynamic voltage and frequency scaling is supported via power control registers
- CPU power modes (Active, Sleep, Deep Sleep) are ARM-standard and nest within the system power modes — both CPUs must request Deep Sleep before the system enters System Deep Sleep
- The high-performance domain can be selectively powered down (PD1 off) while the low-power domain (PD0) remains active — this is the key mechanism for power-gating the CM55/Ethos-U55 between recognition events
- Power mode transitions have documented timing: HP→LP takes ~65 µs typical, LP→HP takes ~49 µs typical, Deep Sleep→Active LP takes ~27 µs typical — factor these into your recognition latency budget

**Learning Check:** Scenario question — "Your terminal has been idle for 30 seconds. The CM33 NNLite detects a face approaching. What sequence of power mode transitions do you initiate, and what are the approximate transition times?" (Answer: Deep Sleep → Active LP → Active HP; ~27 µs + ~49 µs; total ~76 µs before CM55 is running at full speed)

---

#### Subsection 2.1.2: Configure Deep Sleep SRAM Retention

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Comparison table + case study scenario

**Content:**
- Three Deep Sleep variants offer different SRAM retention and power consumption trade-offs:
  - **Deep Sleep-OFF** (SIDDSO): PD1 disabled, no SRAM retention beyond the always-on domain — IBAT typical 20.7 µA; use when the terminal can reload all context from flash on wakeup
  - **Deep Sleep-RAM** (SIDDSRA): PD1 disabled, 64 KB SRAM retention — IBAT typical 21.7 µA; use when you need to retain a small wakeup context (e.g., NNLite model weights, access control state)
  - **Deep Sleep** (SIDDSD): PD1 and CM55 enabled, 64 KB + 1024 KB System SRAM + TCM retention — IBAT typical 41.6 µA; use when you need to retain the full CM55 context for fast wakeup
- SRAM retention is granular — you can select which SRAM blocks to retain, minimizing power while keeping the minimum context needed for your wakeup handler
- The wakeup time from Deep Sleep-RAM and Deep Sleep-OFF to Active is ~25.5 ms (SID14A/SID14B) — significantly longer than from Deep Sleep (~27–40 µs) — factor this into your terminal's response time specification
- For a face recognition terminal: Deep Sleep-RAM with 64 KB retention is appropriate for overnight idle (retain NNLite model and access control state); Deep Sleep with CM55 enabled is appropriate for short inter-recognition pauses (retain full inference context)
- The RTC and 16 backup registers remain active in all deep sleep modes including Hibernate — use backup registers to store critical state that must survive power cycling
- The MCWDT (multi-counter watchdog timer) can serve as a wakeup source from Deep Sleep, clocked by PILO or WCO — configure it to wake the terminal periodically for presence detection polling

**Learning Check:** Decision scenario — given a power budget of 25 µA IBAT and a requirement to retain 64 KB of NNLite model weights during deep sleep, which deep sleep variant do you select? Justify your answer by citing the relevant spec ID and typical current value. (Answer: SIDDSRA at 21.7 µA typical meets the budget; SIDDSD at 41.6 µA exceeds it)

### Section 2.2: Clock Tree Configuration (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll configure the PSOC Edge E8 clock tree — selecting DPLL frequencies, setting clk_hf dividers, and assigning clock paths to peripherals — for each operating state of your face recognition terminal.

---

#### Subsection 2.2.1: Understand Clock Sources and DPLLs

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Annotated clock tree diagram + guided reading

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 provides fully integrated clocks with glitch-free switching: **IHO** (Internal High-Speed Oscillator, fixed 50 MHz ±1%, used as wakeup clock source), **PILO** (Precision Internal Low-Speed Oscillator, 32 kHz, no crystal needed), **ECO** (External Crystal Oscillator, MHz range, pins P19.0/P19.1), **WCO** (Watch Crystal Oscillator, 32.768 kHz, pins P18.0/P18.1)
- Three **DPLLs** are available for clock generation, operating in HP and LP modes: one HP DPLL (50–500 MHz range) and two LP DPLLs (10–500 MHz range in HP mode, up to 350 MHz in LP mode)
- The HP DPLL is optimized for high-speed operation; LP DPLLs are more power-efficient — for your terminal, use the HP DPLL to generate the 320 MHz clock for CM55/Ethos-U55 in HP mode
- 16-bit clock dividers provide fine-grained frequency generation from DPLL outputs — they generate either 1-in-N clocking or approximately 50% duty cycle for analog circuitry
- The IHO is always used as the clock source during wakeup from deep sleep — your firmware must switch to the DPLL after wakeup if full-speed operation is needed
- PLL cold start lock time is ≤60 µs; frequency change lock time is ≤20 µs — add these to your wakeup latency budget when transitioning from deep sleep to active HP mode

**Learning Check:** Fill-in-the-blank — "To generate a 320 MHz clock for the CM55 in HP mode, you configure the _____ DPLL with a source of _____ and an output frequency of _____ MHz. After wakeup from deep sleep, the system initially uses _____ as its clock source before switching to the DPLL."

---

#### Subsection 2.2.2: Build a Clock Configuration Table

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Guided scenario exercise with reference table

**Content:**
- The `clk_hf` root clock dividers distribute clock signals to specific subsystems — key assignments: `clk_hf[0]` → SYSCPUSS/CM33 (max 160 MHz HP, 80 MHz LP), `clk_hf[1]` → APPCPUSS/CM55/U55/GPU/DC (max 320 MHz HP, 140 MHz LP), `clk_hf[2]` → SoCMEM system SRAM (max 240 MHz HP, 110 MHz LP), `clk_hf[3]` → SMIF[0] (max 320 MHz HP, 200 MHz LP), `clk_hf[10]` → SCBs/CAN/TCPWM/SmartI/O (max 80 MHz HP, 40 MHz LP), `clk_hf[12]` → MIPI DSI D-PHY PLL reference (max 51 MHz HP only)
- For active face recognition (HP mode): set DPLL_HP0 to 320 MHz, `clk_hf[1]` divider = 1 (CM55 at 320 MHz), `clk_hf[0]` divider = 2 (CM33 at 160 MHz), `clk_hf[2]` = 240 MHz for SoCMEM
- For idle monitoring (LP mode): set DPLL_LP0 to 160 MHz, `clk_hf[1]` divider = 1 (CM55 at 140 MHz max in LP — use 140 MHz), `clk_hf[0]` divider = 2 (CM33 at 80 MHz), disable `clk_hf[1]` if CM55 is powered down
- For deep sleep: DPLLs are disabled; IHO provides the wakeup clock; `clk_hf[0]` sources from IHO bypass path
- The MIPI DSI D-PHY PLL reference clock (`clk_hf[12]`) is only available in HP mode (max 51 MHz) — the display pipeline cannot operate in LP mode; plan your power mode transitions accordingly
- Dynamic frequency scaling is implemented by writing to power control registers — the PDL provides APIs for this; always follow the documented sequence: change voltage first when scaling down, change frequency first when scaling up

### Section 3.1: ML Accelerator Capabilities and Selection (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll analyze the documented capabilities of the Ethos-U55 and NNLite accelerators and apply a structured decision framework to assign each inference stage of your face recognition pipeline to the correct accelerator.

---

#### Subsection 3.1.1: Analyze Ethos-U55 Capabilities

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Specification deep-dive with annotated feature table

**Content:**
- The Ethos-U55 works in conjunction with the CM55 CPU, running at the same clock frequency (up to 320 MHz in HP mode) — it is a high-performance domain peripheral and requires PD1 to be powered
- Key performance specification: **128 MACs/clock cycle** with 8-bit weight support and weight compression — the CM55 + Ethos-U55 combination is approximately 50× faster than a Cortex-M7 at the same clock frequency for NN inference, and approximately 25× more energy efficient
- Supported NN types: dense (fully connected), convolutional (1D/2D/3D), recurrent (GRU/LSTM) — this covers the full range of architectures used in face recognition (MobileNetV1/V2, RNNoise, and similar)
- Supported NN kernels: convolution (1D/2D/3D), fully-connected, recurrent (GRU/LSTM) — compatible with popular architectures including MobileNetV1, MobileNetV2
- Weight compression reduces memory usage — critical for fitting large face recognition models within the available SRAM budget (up to 5 MB system SRAM + 512 KB TCM for CM55)
- **Use Ethos-U55 for:** full face recognition inference (embedding extraction, liveness detection), any model requiring >4 MACs/cycle throughput, models with convolutional layers that benefit from hardware acceleration, latency-sensitive recognition stages

**Learning Check:** True/False with justification — "The Ethos-U55 can operate while the CM55 domain (PD1) is powered down." (False — Ethos-U55 is a high-performance domain peripheral; it requires PD1 to be active. NNLite in the low-power domain is the correct choice for always-on inference.)

---

#### Subsection 3.1.2: Analyze NNLite Capabilities

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Comparison scenario + specification table

**Content:**
- The NNLite coprocessor works in conjunction with the CM33 CPU, running at the same clock frequency (up to 160 MHz HP, 80 MHz LP) — it is a **low-power domain** peripheral, making it ideal for always-on use cases
- NNLite is an **ACTIVE state peripheral** — it operates when the CM33 is active, not in deep sleep; it is optimized for low-to-moderate complexity ML models such as wake word detection (WWD) and human activity recognition (HAR)
- The MAC unit supports **up to 4 multiplications/additions per clock cycle** — significantly lower throughput than Ethos-U55's 128 MACs/cycle, but at a fraction of the power cost
- Supported kernels: dense (fully connected), 2D convolution, depthwise 2D convolution, pooling (min/max/average), GRU, LSTM, pointwise operations (add/subtract/multiply), re-quantization, activation functions (ReLU, Leaky ReLU, Sigmoid, tanh, Softmax)
- Supported data precisions: 8-bit, 16-bit, and 32-bit for activations and weights; zero weight skipping for efficient computation; compatible with TensorFlow Lite for Microcontrollers
- **Use NNLite for:** lightweight presence/wake detection (is there a face in the frame?), human activity recognition, always-on sensor fusion — workloads that must run continuously while the CM55 domain is powered down

### Section 3.2: Task Partitioning Across CPU Domains (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll apply the IPC block to partition your terminal's firmware tasks across the CM55 and CM33 domains, producing a justified task assignment that references each domain's documented clock frequency, power characteristics, and peripheral access.

---

#### Subsection 3.2.1: Apply the IPC Block for Task Synchronization

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Architecture diagram walkthrough + scenario exercise

**Content:**
- The **IPC (Inter-Processor Communication) block** is the hardware mechanism for synchronizing tasks between the CM55 and CM33 — it provides semaphore-based signaling without requiring shared memory polling
- The CM55 domain is the high-performance domain: it has direct access to the Ethos-U55, the 2.5D GPU, the display controller, SMIF (external flash), USB, and the 4-channel HPDMA (AXI bus, 128-byte FIFO per channel) — use it for compute-intensive, latency-sensitive tasks
- The CM33 domain is the low-power domain: it has direct access to NNLite, all 12 SCBs, I3C, CAN FD, Ethernet, Crypto, and the 2×16-channel DMA (AHB bus) — use it for always-on sensing, communications, and security operations
- Both CPUs can access resources in each other's domains via a bus bridge — but cross-domain access adds latency and power; minimize it by assigning tasks to the domain that owns the relevant peripheral
- The CM33 runs TF-M in the Secure Processing Environment (SPE) — all security operations (key management, attestation, secure storage) must execute on the CM33 in the SPE, not on the CM55
- IPC workflow for face recognition: CM33 NNLite detects presence → CM33 signals CM55 via IPC → CM55 wakes up, runs full face recognition via Ethos-U55 → CM55 sends result to CM33 via IPC → CM33 makes access control decision and actuates door relay

#### Subsection 3.2.2: Partition Memory for Multi-Core Operation

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Memory map diagram + guided reading

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 provides multiple memory types with different access characteristics: **512 KB RRAM** (35 ns read access, 128-bit reads with word caching, in the low-power domain — use for CM33 code and security assets), **up to 5 MB system SRAM (SoCMEM)** (shared, accessible by both CPUs via bus bridge), **1 MB SRAM coupled with CM33** (low-power domain, fast CM33 access), **512 KB TCM for CM55** (256 KB instruction + 256 KB data, 0-wait-state — use for time-critical CM55 code and inference buffers)
- The SMIF interfaces (2×, each with 32 KB cache) support XIP (execute-in-place) from external flash up to 64 MB — use for storing large ML models that don't fit in internal SRAM; the 32 KB cache reduces repeated flash access latency
- SRAM wait states depend on the access path and operating frequency — the CM55 accessing SoCMEM at 320 MHz incurs more wait states than accessing its own TCM; place your most latency-sensitive inference buffers in TCM
- 5120 bytes of RRAM are dedicated to OTP use cases including security asset storage — this is where device keys and provisioning data live; do not use this region for application data
- For your terminal: store the NNLite presence detection model in CM33-coupled SRAM; store the full face recognition model in SoCMEM or external flash (XIP via SMIF); store inference input/output buffers in CM55 TCM for lowest latency
- The DMA controllers handle data movement autonomously: HPDMA (CM55 domain) for large transfers (image frames from USB/SMIF to SoCMEM); DMA (CM33 domain) for small transactional transfers (ADC results, I2C sensor data)

**Learning Check:** Memory allocation scenario — given a face recognition model requiring 2 MB of weights and a presence detection model requiring 128 KB, plus 512 KB of inference buffers for the CM55, describe where you would place each component and why, referencing the documented memory types and their access characteristics.

## Module 4: Security Architecture & PSA Compliance (14 minutes)

**Module Overview:** Your face recognition terminal is an access control device — security isn't optional. This module walks you through the PSOC Edge E8's security architecture, helps you select the right EPC security level for your application, and shows you how to configure TrustZone SPE/NSPE partitioning with TF-M.

---

### Section 4.1: Selecting EPC Level and Configuring TrustZone (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll select the correct part number (EPC2 vs EPC4) for your PSA compliance requirement and specify which firmware components belong in the Secure Processing Environment (SPE) versus the Non-Secure Processing Environment (NSPE).

---

#### Subsection 4.1.1: Select EPC2 vs EPC4 Security Level

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Decision framework + ordering information table

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 is available in two security configurations: **EPC2** (Infineon Edge Protect Category 2, supporting SESIP/PSA Level 2) and **EPC4** (Infineon Edge Protect Category 4, supporting SESIP/PSA Level 3 + secure enclave)
- Both EPC levels include: hardware crypto accelerator, TrustZone isolation, Infineon proprietary MPU/MPC/PPCs, secure boot with hardware-based Root of Trust (RoT), DICE mechanisms for device attestation, and off-the-shelf TF-M + mbedTLS
- **EPC4 adds:** a lock-step secure enclave with runtime services (PSA-compliant cryptographic services, key management, attestation, secure storage), tamper detection, side-channel attack (SCA) mitigation, and fault injection attack protection — EPC4 targets PSA Level 3 + ISE certification (pending)
- For an access control terminal: **EPC4 is the appropriate choice** — access control devices store biometric templates and cryptographic keys that require the highest level of hardware protection; PSA Level 3 + secure enclave provides the isolation needed for production deployment
- Part number selection: EPC4 variants include suffix "S4" in the part number (e.g., PSE846GPS4DBZQ3 = E84, BGA-220, 5120 KB SRAM, U55, GFX, EPC4); EPC2 variants use "S2" (e.g., PSE846GPS2DBZQ3)
- Secure boot with hardware-based RoT is enabled on both EPC levels — the boot ROM authenticates the OEM application before execution; the boot source is selected via the P17.6 pin state (Low = OEM_APP_ADDRESS, High = OEM_ALT_APP_ADDRESS)

**Learning Check:** Part number selection exercise — given the requirement for PSA Level 3 compliance, Ethos-U55 support, graphics capability, 5120 KB system SRAM, and BGA-220 package, identify the correct part number from the ordering table and explain why EPC4 is required over EPC2 for this application.

---

#### Subsection 4.1.2: Configure TrustZone SPE/NSPE Partitioning

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Security architecture diagram walkthrough + partitioning exercise

**Content:**
- The CM33 is a TrustZone-enabled core with two processing environments: the **Secure Processing Environment (SPE)** and the **Non-Secure Processing Environment (NSPE)** — the CM55 operates exclusively in NSPE and accesses TF-M services via PSA API calls over IPC
- **SPE contains:** Infineon-provided TF-M (Trusted Firmware-M) implementation, Extended Boot firmware, hardware crypto accelerator access, secure key storage, and (EPC4 only) secure enclave runtime services — this is where your biometric template encryption keys and access control credentials live
- **NSPE contains:** your application code on both CM33 and CM55, middleware, RTOS tasks, and any code that does not require direct access to secure assets — face recognition inference, display rendering, and peripheral drivers all run in NSPE
- TF-M services are accessed from NSPE via the PSA API — your CM55 face recognition code calls TF-M to encrypt/decrypt biometric templates, generate attestation tokens, and access secure storage, without ever touching the secure keys directly
- Infineon proprietary protection units (MPCs and PPCs) enforce memory and peripheral access control using secured/non-secured address aliases and protection contexts — the PSOC Edge E8 supports up to 8 protection contexts
- For your terminal: configure the SPE to contain TF-M + biometric key management + access control credential storage; configure the NSPE to contain face recognition inference (CM55), display rendering (CM55), presence detection (CM33), and all peripheral drivers; use PSA API calls across the SPE/NSPE boundary for all security operations

**Learning Check:** Partitioning scenario — given a list of six firmware components (TF-M key management service, face embedding extraction model, door relay GPIO driver, biometric template encryption routine, MIPI-DSI display driver, device attestation token generator), assign each to SPE or NSPE and justify each assignment with reference to the documented security architecture.

### Section 5.1: Display Pipeline and Serial Peripheral Configuration (14 minutes)

**Section Overview:** You'll configure the MIPI-DSI display pipeline for your terminal's status display and select the correct SCB peripheral mode for each external interface, identifying the hardware constraints that affect your PCB design.

---

#### Subsection 5.1.1: Configure the MIPI-DSI Display Pipeline

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Pipeline diagram walkthrough + hardware constraint checklist

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 graphics subsystem consists of: a **2.5D GPU** (rendering engine, up to 200 MHz in HP mode), a **Display Controller (DC)** (multi-plane composition, frame buffer compression), and a **MIPI-DSI host controller** (2-lane output, 500–1500 Mbps per lane) — frames flow GPU → DC → DSI host → display panel
- The display controller supports up to **1024×768 resolution at 60 fps**, up to 24 bits per pixel, with 4-layer alpha blending and 2× frame buffer compression via YUV 4:2:0 color space conversion
- Both **DBI (command mode)** and **DPI (video mode)** standards are supported over the DSI interface; MIPI DBI Types A, B, and C are also available for displays with integrated frame buffers
- **Critical hardware constraint:** If you use the graphics feature, you must connect the **DSI.REXT pin (J6 on BGA-220) to a 200 Ω resistor to ground** — if graphics is not used, tie DSI.REXT to ground. Failure to do this will cause DSI PHY malfunction
- The display pipeline operates only in **HP mode** — `clk_hf[12]` (MIPI DSI D-PHY PLL reference, max 51 MHz) is only available in HP mode; plan your power mode transitions to ensure the display is updated before entering LP or deep sleep
- GPIO routing for DSI: dedicated pins DSI.CLKN/P (A4/A5), DSI.DATA0N/P (B4/B3), DSI.DATA1N/P (A3/A2) — these are fixed-function pins, not routed through HSIOM; the GPU/DC alternate functions for DBI mode route through P16/P17 via HSIOM settings ACT #13, #14, #15

**Learning Check:** Hardware design checklist — identify three hardware design decisions you must make for the MIPI-DSI interface (resistor value and pin, power mode constraint, DBI vs DPI mode selection) and explain the consequence of getting each one wrong.

---

#### Subsection 5.1.2: Select and Configure SCB Peripheral Modes

**Duration:** 7 minutes
**Format:** Interface mapping table + constraint identification exercise

**Content:**
- The PSOC Edge E8 has **12 SCBs**: SCB1–SCB11 can be configured as I2C master/slave, UART, or SPI master/slave; **SCB0 is special** — it only supports I2C slave mode and SPI slave mode, and it can operate in System Deep Sleep with an external clock (the only SCB that can do this)
- **SCB0 constraint for your terminal:** Use SCB0 only for interfaces that must remain active during deep sleep (e.g., an I2C slave interface to a host controller that polls the terminal during sleep); do not use SCB0 as a UART or I2C master
- I2C mode: up to 1 Mbps (Fast Mode Plus), full multi-master arbitration — use for sensor buses (e.g., IR sensor, temperature sensor); route via HSIOM ACT #7 settings
- UART mode: up to 8 Mbps in LP mode, 16 Mbps in HP mode — use for debug console (UART) and host communication; route via HSIOM ACT #8 settings
- SPI mode: standard SCBs up to 25 Mbps; one SCB supports high-speed mode up to 50 Mbps (Fscb/4 with Fscb max 200 MHz) — use for high-speed SPI flash or display interfaces; route via HSIOM ACT #6 settings
- **GPIO HSIOM routing:** Each SCB signal must be routed to a specific GPIO port via the HSIOM bitfield — for example, SCB1 I2C SCL routes to P9.3 (ACT #7), SCB1 SPI MOSI routes to P9.2 (ACT #6); consult Table 12/13 in the datasheet for the complete routing table; the ModusToolbox Device Configurator automates this but you must verify pin conflicts manually

**Learning Check:** Peripheral configuration plan exercise — given four interface requirements (MIPI-DSI display, I2C sensor bus that must work in deep sleep, SPI external flash at 25 Mbps, UART debug at 115200 baud), map each to the correct PSOC Edge E8 peripheral, identify the GPIO port and HSIOM setting, and flag any constraints that affect the hardware design.

## Course Design Notes

### Pedagogical Approach

This course uses a **task-centered instructional design** model, where every subsection is anchored to a decision or action Alex will perform on the job — not a topic to be memorized. The sequence moves from architectural orientation (what is this chip?) through configuration decisions (how do I set it up?) to integration (how do I bring it all together?), mirroring the actual firmware development workflow.

**Spaced retrieval** is built into the learning check questions, which require Alex to apply information from earlier subsections in new contexts — for example, the deep sleep SRAM retention exercise requires applying both the power mode knowledge from Section 2.1 and the current consumption specs from the electrical specifications.

**Worked examples** are used throughout to ground abstract specifications in the face recognition terminal context — every specification (128 MACs/cycle, 20.7 µA IBAT, 25.5 ms wakeup time) is immediately connected to a terminal design decision.

### Assessment Strategy

- **Formative assessments** (learning checks) are embedded in every subsection — they are low-stakes, immediately corrective, and designed to surface misconceptions before they compound
- **Summative assessment** (5-question final) uses scenario-based questions that require synthesis across modules — no question can be answered by recalling a single fact; each requires applying multiple concepts
- **Passing threshold** of 4/5 (80%) reflects the high-stakes nature of firmware architecture decisions — a misconfigured power domain or wrong EPC level has real consequences in production

### Accessibility

- All interactive diagrams include text-based alternatives (tables, bulleted lists) for screen reader compatibility
- Video content includes closed captions and full transcripts
- Color is never the sole differentiator in diagrams — patterns and labels are used alongside color coding
- All downloadable job aids are provided in both PDF and accessible HTML formats

### Delivery Format Rationale

- **Interactive block diagrams** are used for architecture content (Modules 1, 3) because spatial relationships between blocks are the core learning — static images are insufficient
- **Comparison tables** are used for accelerator selection and power mode content because the decision is inherently comparative
- **Scenario exercises** are used for partitioning and configuration tasks because these are the actual job tasks Alex will perform
- **Annotated schematics** are used for hardware constraint content (DSI.REXT, power supply diagrams) because the constraint is physical and must be visualized
